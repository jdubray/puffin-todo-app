# Vision & Principles

## The Problem with Every To-Do App

Every to-do app lies to you. It tells you that productivity is about managing a list. It is not.

Productivity is about **attention** — knowing where your energy should go right now, remembering the context behind each commitment, and feeling the satisfaction of meaningful progress.

Conventional apps give you a blank text field and a checkbox. The metaphor is a grocery list. Pulse rejects that metaphor entirely.

---

## The Core Metaphor: Living Organisms

In Pulse, a task is a **living entity** — not a static record.

Like a living thing, a task:

- **Is born** with intention (why does this exist?)
- **Grows** as you feed it context, links, notes, and voice memos
- **Has a state** — dormant, active, urgent, blocked, complete
- **Connects** to other tasks and the outside world
- **Has memory** — it remembers where it came from and what came before it
- **Dies gracefully** — completion is an event, not a deletion

This metaphor shapes every design decision. A task that sits untouched for two weeks starts to look different from a task you worked on this morning. A task with ten linked resources looks different from a bare note.

## The Four North Stars

### 1. Personal

The app should feel like it was built specifically for you.

- It learns your vocabulary, your naming patterns, your scheduling preferences
- It surfaces patterns you did not know you had ("You always tackle email on Wednesday mornings")
- The visual environment can be customized to match your aesthetic
- Your memory vault is yours — portable, private, local-first

### 2. Innovative

The interface pushes beyond what any existing to-do app has done.

- Tasks live on a **spatial canvas**, not a flat list
- Relationships between tasks are visible
- Time is a first-class dimension — tasks have a past, a present, and a future
- The memory layer turns completed work into a searchable, resurfaceable knowledge base
- Voice is a primary input mode, not an afterthought

### 3. Productive

Reducing friction is sacred. Every interaction should have the fewest possible steps.

- The command palette (`⌘K` / `Ctrl+K`) reaches everything
- Voice capture works from anywhere in the app
- Resources are linked with drag-and-drop
- Tasks can be emailed in, emailed out, and calendared with one action
- Focus Mode removes everything except the task at hand

### 4. Fun

Work should not feel like suffering. The app should make completion feel rewarding.

- Completing a hard task triggers a satisfying animation — a "victory lap"
- Streaks and momentum scores give positive reinforcement without being gamey
- The visual canvas has **seasons** that change with the real calendar
- Voice interaction has personality — it responds with warmth, not robotic confirmation
- The app remembers your best days and can show you what you've accomplished

## Anti-Patterns (Things We Are NOT Building)

| Anti-pattern | Why we reject it |
|---|---|
| Flat checkbox list | Destroys context; makes all tasks feel equal |
| Due-date-only urgency | Urgency is multi-dimensional; deadlines are one signal |
| Archive as burial | Completed tasks are your history, not your trash |
| Notification spam | Interruptions kill focus; resurfacing should feel natural |
| Gamification theater | Badges without meaning are noise |
| Sync-first architecture | Local-first means it works perfectly offline, always |

## The Feeling We Are Designing Toward

> You open the app. Your canvas shows the landscape of your commitments — some glowing with urgency, some sitting quietly, some clustered around a project you care about. You speak: *"Add: prepare slides for the Q2 kickoff, due Friday, link the Figma file."* The task blooms into existence on the canvas. You click into a task you started yesterday, and the app shows you the webpage you were reading, the notes you dictated, and — quietly in the corner — a similar task you completed six months ago. You finish the task. The canvas shifts. A small celebration. A new memory is formed.
>
> That felt good. You want to do it again.

## Design Language

**Aesthetic direction:** Organic modernism — clean enough to focus, warm enough to inhabit.

- **Color:** Deep background (near-black), luminous task cards, accent colors per project
- **Motion:** Purposeful, never decorative. Tasks breathe; they don't bounce.
- **Typography:** High-contrast, generous line height. Reading a task should be effortless.
- **Sound (optional):** Subtle ambient audio in Focus Mode; satisfying completion sounds
- **Seasons:** The canvas shifts visually with real-world seasons (spring bloom, summer clarity, autumn warmth, winter stillness)
